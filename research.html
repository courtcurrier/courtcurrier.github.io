COURTNEY M. CURRIER

how do grassy ecosystems adapt to a changing world?

Research Publications Fieldwork Paintings About

I am an ecosystem ecologist studying savannas and grasslands, with a focus on how these systems respond and adapt to disturbances such as fire, grazing, and climate change. The sites where I work are typically dry and already shaped by these disturbances, which define the structure and function of these ecosystems. I am particularly interested in the effects of novel disturbance regimes that exceed historical magnitudes or durations. My research is empirical, combining long-term and large-scale field experiments with advanced laboratory analyses. My goal is to understand the role of grassy ecosystems in mitigating climate change, particularly through their influence on carbon cycling. I investigate both changes in carbon stocks and persistence, as well as the underlying mechanisms driving carbon fluxes, such as microbial activity and interactions with other nutrient cycles.

Current Projects

Disturance x climate controls on soil carbon storage and persistence

We investigate how fire frequency, grazing intensity, and precipitation shape soil carbon storage and long-term persistence in grassland ecosystems.

Acclimation of biogeochemical cycles to global change

This project studies how nitrogen and carbon cycles adjust under novel climate and disturbance regimes.

Mechanistic microbial feedbacks to C and N cycling

Examining how microbial biomass, community composition, and enzymatic activity respond to fire and grazing.

Grassland resilience across aridity gradients

We measure ecosystem recovery, plant composition, and productivity along gradients of dryness.
